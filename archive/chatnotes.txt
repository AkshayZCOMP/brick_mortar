Conceptually, yes: you would have multiple displacement unknowns sampled at the same (x)-stations, one for each “phase” you want to represent.
That does not mean you are putting “two materials in the same place.” It means you are representing two different average kinematic fields:
•	(u_{f,0}(x)): axial displacement of tow row 0
•	(u_{f,1}(x)): axial displacement of tow row 1
•	(u_m(x)): axial displacement of the matrix “backbone” (or interstitial matrix)
In the discrete model, you approximate each field at the same node locations (x_i), so at each (x_i) you have separate DOFs:
•	(u_{0,i}), (u_{1,i}), (u_{m,i})
How they interact
•	Along (x): each phase has its own axial stiffness chain
o	tow axial elements connect (u_{r,i}) to (u_{r,i+1})
o	matrix axial elements connect (u_{m,i}) to (u_{m,i+1})
•	Between phases at the same (x_i): you add coupling that represents matrix shear transfer
o	shear coupling connects (u_{r,i}) to (u_{m,i})
That coupling is what produces shear-lag behavior: if the tow wants to move differently than the matrix at the same (x), the coupling resists that mismatch.
Why this “fills the break” naturally
A tow “break” in row 0 is simply removing the tow axial connection(s) in that interval. The matrix chain is still continuous, so load can detour:
[
\text{tow} \rightarrow \text{(shear coupling)} \rightarrow \text{matrix axial chain} \rightarrow \text{(shear coupling)} \rightarrow \text{tow}
]
That is a much more physical representation of “the gap is filled with resin” than inserting an ad hoc bridge spring, because bridging emerges from the matrix network you already have.
Important clarification
You do not need 2 DOFs (like (u_x, u_y)) to accomplish “filled with matrix.” A single axial DOF for the matrix field (u_m(x)) is enough to capture axial bridging plus shear-lag in the classic reduced-order sense (this is essentially what Beyerlein–Landis are doing, just discretized differently).
If you later want to capture transverse deformation explicitly, that is when you add additional DOFs (e.g., (u_y)), but that is a bigger step and not required just to model resin filling a break.

According to a document from **1999**, Beyerlein and Landis build a *finite-element-based shear-lag* model where (i) **fibers are 1D axial springs** and (ii) the **matrix between consecutive fibers is modeled with a pair of rectangular finite elements**, while still **neglecting transverse displacements** (so each rectangular matrix element has **four axial DOFs**).

Below is what you are doing now versus what they are doing, and what that changes.

---

## What you are doing now (your current spring network)

**Topology**

* Two “tow rows,” each a 1D chain of 2-node axial springs along (x).
* “Matrix” between rows is represented as **lumped coupling springs** between the two rows at matching (x_i) nodes (your `etype="shear"`).

**How a break is represented**

* You remove/replace tow axial springs in a break interval in row 0.
* If “filled,” you replace the missing tow segment with a different axial spring segment (`etype="tow_bridge"`), i.e., an *ad hoc* axial bridging stiffness.

**Kinematics**

* One DOF per node: axial displacement only (so everything—axial tow, “shear,” bridge—is ultimately a scalar spring connecting two axial DOFs).

**Outputs you can interpret cleanly**

* Tow axial stress (from tow elements only).
* Interface shear “traction” proxy (from coupling springs), but you must keep units consistent.

**What is implicitly missing**

* A *separate matrix displacement field* (there is no matrix axial DOF chain), so the matrix cannot carry axial load except through whatever you hard-code as “bridge.”

---

## What Beyerlein–Landis are doing

### 1) They explicitly model **both fiber and matrix displacement fields**

They have **fiber nodes** and **matrix nodes** (both with axial displacements), and their governing equations couple fiber and matrix displacements. Their normalized formulation introduces the key stiffness ratio

[
q = \frac{E_m A_m}{E_f A_f},
]

i.e., **matrix axial stiffness relative to fiber axial stiffness**.

This is the central physics difference: your present model does not have a native (q); you are approximating “matrix axial load carrying” by inventing a bridge stiffness.

### 2) Their matrix is not a single nodal connector; it’s a **rectangular-element stencil**

They state: fibers are **1D axial springs** with stiffness (E_f A_f/\Delta x), and the matrix region between consecutive fiber elements is represented by **a pair of rectangular finite elements**; since transverse displacements are neglected, each element has **four DOFs** (axial only).

Implication: their matrix coupling is **distributed and FE-consistent**, not a single 2-node lumped spring. That tends to reduce mesh artifacts and gives a cleaner route to matrix stress recovery.

### 3) They recover **fiber axial stress and matrix axial + shear stress** under shear-lag assumptions

They explicitly frame the model as providing average fiber axial stress and average matrix shear stress between adjacent fiber/matrix nodes.

Implication: you can extend your model to output similar quantities, but you need either (a) their matrix-element stencil or (b) an explicit matrix DOF chain + coupling.

### 4) Break handling and boundary conditions are different in spirit

They construct **unit “break-opening” solutions** (fiber break or matrix break), then superimpose a far-field strain solution to enforce “traction-free breaks” at the damaged sites.

Implication: they do not “delete elements and solve KU=f for a big mesh” in the same way; they use influence functions/superposition to handle many breaks efficiently.

### 5) Computational scaling is aimed at “many breaks”

They highlight that the compute effort is tied to solving for weights associated with damaged elements; the method is designed to remain efficient as damage accumulates.

Implication: your direct solve scales with the total discretization length and number of rows; their approach scales primarily with the number/configuration of breaks (the whole point is large-scale failure simulations).

---

## Practical implications for your results

### A) Why your shear traction can look “insane”

In your current lumped-spring coupling, shear traction magnitude is extremely sensitive to (i) unit consistency and (ii) the chosen connector stiffness. Beyerlein–Landis avoid part of this sensitivity by having a matrix formulation that is internally consistent and parameterized through (G_m), (E_m), and (q) in a structured way.

### B) Your “filled break” is currently a modeling choice; in their framework it is endogenous

In your model, whether the gap is “filled” depends on whether you insert `tow_bridge` and what stiffness you assign. In their framework, “matrix carries axial load” is governed by (q = E_m A_m/(E_f A_f)) and the matrix field itself, so bridging emerges naturally from the matrix representation.

### C) Their model is still not “full 2D continuum”

Even with rectangular elements, they explicitly neglect transverse displacements.
So it’s “2D” in *topology/stencil*, not 2D in kinematics. That is why it remains shear-lag-like and computationally light.

You do not need matrix nodes to handle ply-to-ply transverse shifts or “half-overlap onto two neighbors.” You can do that cleanly in the tow-only graph by splitting interface stiffness across the two neighbors.
That said, adding matrix nodes is a legitimate next step if your roadmap includes matrix-dominated phenomena (gap filling, debonding/cohesive behavior, matrix stress recovery). The question is when to pay the complexity cost.
What you gain by adding matrix nodes
Matrix nodes (i.e., explicit matrix DOFs) let you represent the matrix as a field rather than as an “already-eliminated” medium:
	A real resin ligament in a break
	Instead of a single “bridge spring,” you can represent a finite-length matrix segment with its own DOFs and (if desired) its own axial stiffness along x.
	This reduces “lumped” behavior and makes stress/traction recovery more interpretable.
	Clean many-to-one coupling for shifted plies
	If an upper tow overlaps two lower tows, you can connect both to a matrix node under that tow segment.
	This avoids ad hoc stiffness splitting rules and makes energy bookkeeping easier.
	A path to cohesive laws and damage
	Cohesive/interface elements often want an explicit traction–separation relationship at an interface. A matrix/interface DOF layer makes that more direct.
	Better stress recovery in the matrix
	In tow-only models, “matrix stress” is inferred from spring forces. With matrix DOFs you can compute strains/stresses more consistently.
What you pay (the real drawbacks)
	DOF count increases materially
	Tow-only: n_"rows" ⋅n_"nodes" 
	With one matrix node per interface per x-node: add (n_"rows" -1)⋅n_"nodes" 
	In multi-ply, this grows fast.
	More chances to over-stiffen by double-counting
	If you add matrix nodes, you typically must remove the direct tow–tow shear springs at the same location, otherwise you create parallel load paths and artificially increase stiffness.
	You must define matrix geometry more carefully
	Areas/thicknesses (b_"eff" ,t_"eff" , ligament area) matter more, and poor choices can dominate the response.
	Conditioning gets worse
	Very stiff tow springs plus much softer matrix springs can lead to ill-conditioned systems. It is solvable, but you will notice it.
What I recommend for your workflow
Do not add matrix nodes everywhere yet. Add them only where they buy you something immediately, and keep the rest as tow-only coupling. Concretely:
Stage 1 (now): Tow-only graph, validated
	Keep your current model for ply shifts: connect to 1 or 2 neighbors and split k_shby overlap fraction so total coupling stays physical.
	Goal: confirm you can reproduce expected far-field stress (~E_f ε_0) and reasonable transfer peaks.
Stage 2 (next): Add matrix nodes only in tow breaks
	This is where you “need them anyway,” because a break is literally “tow replaced by matrix over a finite length.”
	You can add a chain of matrix nodes along xover the gap and give it axial stiffness k=E_m A_"bridge" /Δx.
	Everything else stays as before.
Stage 3 (later): Matrix nodes at ply interfaces (optional)
	Only if you want more realistic inter-ply traction distribution, cohesive behavior, or matrix stress fields.
	If you do this, you replace direct tow–tow shear springs with tow–matrix and matrix–tow couplings.
